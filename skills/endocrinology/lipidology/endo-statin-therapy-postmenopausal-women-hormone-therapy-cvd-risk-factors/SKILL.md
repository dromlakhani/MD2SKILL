# Statin therapy for postmenopausal women on hormone therapy with cardiovascular risk factors

## STEP 1 — Gather Information
Collect menopausal status, hormone therapy type (oral/transdermal, estrogen ± progestin) and duration, traditional cardiovascular risk factors (age, smoking, hypertension, diabetes, LDL-C, family history of premature ASCVD), lipid panel (LDL-C, HDL-C, triglycerides), calculate 10-year ASCVD risk using Pooled Cohort Equations, and assess for risk-enhancing factors (elevated Lp(a) ≥50 mg/dL, metabolic syndrome, chronic kidney disease, inflammatory conditions). End with action: determine 10-year ASCVD risk category and presence of risk-enhancing factors.

## STEP 2 — Rule In / Rule Out
Rule in statin consideration if the patient has at least one traditional CVD risk factor (e.g., LDL-C ≥70 mg/dL, hypertension, diabetes, smoking, family history of premature ASCVD) or any risk-enhancing factor; otherwise rule out statin initiation for primary prevention in this population. End with decision: proceed to risk stratification if risk factors present, else consider statin not indicated.

## STEP 3 — Classify or Stratify
If 10-year ASCVD risk ≥20% or established ASCVD: high-intensity statin indicated. If risk 7.5%-19.9%: moderate-intensity statin indicated. If risk 5%-7.4% with ≥1 risk-enhancing factor: consider moderate-intensity statin. If risk <5% and no risk-enhancing factors: statin not indicated. End with action: select statin intensity or defer therapy.

## STEP 4 — Decide
Initiate the selected statin intensity after discussing risks/benefits with the patient; if statin not indicated, emphasize lifestyle modification and repeat risk assessment in 3-5 years. End with action: prescribe statin or advise lifestyle-only approach.

## Clinical Guardrails / Mimics / Pitfalls
Do not initiate statin in pregnancy or when trying to become pregnant; hormone therapy increases venous thromboembolism and stroke risk, which statin does not fully mitigate; monitor for statin-associated muscle symptoms, especially with concomitant CYP3A4 inhibitors (e.g., clarithromycin, azole antifungals); avoid statin in active liver disease or unexplained persistent ALT/AST elevation; note that hormone therapy may raise triglycerides—if TG ≥500 mg/dL, address hypertriglyceridemia before or alongside statin therapy; do not rely solely on statin to offset hormone‑related thrombotic risk.

## Concrete Clinical Example
A 58‑year‑old postmenopausal woman on oral estrogen‑progestin HRT for 4 years presents for routine care. She has hypertension (treated), LDL-C 145 mg/dL, HDL-C 48 mg/dL, triglycerides 160 mg/dL, former smoker (quit 5 years ago), no diabetes. Her 10‑year ASCVD risk is 14%. She has one traditional risk factor (hypertension) and elevated LDL‑C, so statin is ruled in. Risk 7.5‑19.9% indicates moderate‑intensity statin. She is started on atorvastatin 20 mg daily after discussing benefits (≈30% relative CVD risk reduction) and potential side effects.
