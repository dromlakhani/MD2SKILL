# Against routine 25(OH)D testing in adults under 50

## STEP 1 — Gather Information
Collect patient age, presence of symptoms, and any established indications for 25(OH)D testing (e.g., hypocalcemia, malabsorption syndromes, osteoporosis, pregnancy, chronic kidney disease, or medications affecting vitamin D metabolism). Confirm the patient is under 50 years old, asymptomatic, and has no such indications.

## STEP 2 — Rule In / Rule Out
If the patient is 50 years or older OR has any established indication for 25(OH)D testing, proceed to consider testing per other guidelines; otherwise, rule out routine 25(OH)D testing.

## STEP 3 — Classify or Stratify
Classify the scenario as "routine screening not indicated" when age <50 and asymptomatic without indications; further stratify by noting that subgroups such as obesity, dark complexion, or pregnancy still do not warrant routine testing absent specific clinical indications.

## STEP 4 — Decide
Do not order a routine 25(OH)D test; instead, discuss that empiric vitamin D supplementation is not recommended based on testing results and advise following age-appropriate dietary reference intakes unless other clinical indications arise.

## Clinical Guardrails / Mimics / Pitfalls
Do not use 25(OH)D levels to guide supplementation decisions in this population; avoid testing solely for wellness or preventive screening; be aware that testing may lead to unnecessary supplementation, increased costs, and potential harm from excess vitamin D; note that absence of testing does not preclude addressing symptomatic vitamin D deficiency if clinical signs develop.

## Concrete Clinical Example
A 45-year-old man with fatigue and normal diet requests a 25(OH)D test for "optimal health." He has no symptoms of bone pain, fractures, or known malabsorption. After confirming age <50 and no established indications, the clinician advises against routine testing, explains that empiric supplementation is not recommended without indication, and suggests focusing on balanced diet and sun safety.
