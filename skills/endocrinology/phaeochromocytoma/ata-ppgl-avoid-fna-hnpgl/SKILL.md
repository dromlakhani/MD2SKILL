# Avoid fine needle cytology for head and neck paraganglioma diagnosis

## STEP 1 — Gather Information
Collect clinical presentation (neck mass, pulsatile tinnitus, cranial nerve palsies), high-resolution MRI/CT of skull base and neck, biochemical tests (plasma-free fractionated metanephrines, normetanephrines, 3-methoxytyramine), and assess for hereditary risk (family history, multifocality) to confirm suspicion of head and neck paraganglioma.

## STEP 2 — Rule In / Rule Out
Is the lesion suspected to be a head and neck paraganglioma based on imaging and clinical features? If yes, proceed to avoid fine needle cytology; if no, consider fine needle cytology for alternative diagnoses (e.g., thyroid nodule, lymph node metastasis).

## STEP 3 — Classify or Stratify
Subclassify the suspected head and neck paraganglioma as functional (elevated plasma-free metanephrines/normetanephrines or 3-methoxytyramine) or nonfunctional (normal biomarkers) to counsel on procedural risks.

## STEP 4 — Decide
Do not perform fine needle cytology; rely on imaging characteristics, genetic testing (SDHD/SDHB), and definitive surgical pathology after resection for diagnosis.

## Clinical Guardrails / Mimics / Pitfalls
Avoid fine needle cytology due to risk of hypertensive crisis or hemorrhage in functional tumors, high false-negative rates, and frequent misdiagnosis (e.g., thyroid paragangliomas mistaken for follicular neoplasms); do not substitute with core needle biopsy without hemodynamic precautions.

## Concrete Clinical Example
A 48-year-old man presents with a left neck mass and pulsatile tinnitus. MRI shows a enhancing carotid body lesion. Plasma-free metanephrines and normetanephrines are normal. Suspected nonfunctional head and neck paraganglioma. Per guideline, fine needle cytology is avoided; instead, SDHD genetic testing is ordered and surgical resection is planned after alpha-blockade.
