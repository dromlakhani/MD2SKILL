# Prescribe specific healthy eating habits for pediatric obesity prevention/treatment

## STEP 1 — Gather Information
Collect age, sex, BMI percentile, dietary intake (frequency of sugar-sweetened beverages, sports drinks, fruit drinks, fast food, processed snacks, fruit juice), and family eating habits during well-child or sick-child visit.

## STEP 2 — Rule In / Rule Out
If BMI percentile ≥85th (overweight/obese) or patient reports excessive juice (>4 oz/day <6 yr, >8 oz/day ≥6 yr) or frequent calorie-dense nutrient-poor foods, proceed to step 3; otherwise provide general healthy eating counseling and conclude.

## STEP 3 — Classify or Stratify
Classify by age for juice limits: <6 months (no juice), 6 months–6 years (limit to 4–6 oz/day), >6 years (limit to 8–12 oz/day); also classify intake of sugary beverages/snacks as high (>1 serving/day) or moderate/low.

## STEP 4 — Decide
Prescribe: avoid sugar-sweetened beverages, sports drinks, fruit drinks, fast foods, high-fat/high-sodium processed foods, calorie-dense snacks; replace fruit juice with whole fruits per age limits; encourage water and regular meals; provide portion control education and involve family.

## Clinical Guardrails / Mimics / Pitfalls
Do not rely solely on BMI without dietary assessment; avoid recommending fruit juice as a healthy substitute for whole fruit; do not ignore cultural dietary preferences when suggesting alternatives; avoid overly restrictive diets that may cause nutrient deficiencies or disordered eating.

## Concrete Clinical Example
A 7‑year‑old with BMI at the 90th percentile reports drinking 12 oz of apple juice daily and eating fast food three times weekly. Counsel to limit juice to ≤8 oz/day, replace excess juice with whole fruit, substitute fast food meals with home‑prepared meals containing vegetables and lean protein, and eliminate sugary snacks.

**Source:** Pediatric Obesity Assessment, Treatment, and Prevention: An Endocrine Society Clinical Practice Guideline, Endocrine Society, 2017, DOI:10.1210/jc.2016-2573
